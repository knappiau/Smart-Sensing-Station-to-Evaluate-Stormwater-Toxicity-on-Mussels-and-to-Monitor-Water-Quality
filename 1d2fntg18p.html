Actual Sensor Development

In order to productize the PCB sensor for in situ use, the substrate material, currently FR-4, will be replaced with liquid crystal polymer (LCP), which is a modern PCB material with a moisture permeability similar to glass.  This will allow the sensor to function when continuously submerged in water.  A low-cost, integrated sensor interface circuit will be developed to measure the dependent sensor’s capacitance, which possesses a resonant peak between 40 MHz and 50 MHz and transmitted to the MCU (Fig. \ref{894273}).

Sensor Data Analysis and Combination With Mussel Behavior

We will use different analytical techniques to relate the sensor's data to the mussel behavior and hence toxicity, such as partial least square regression (PSLR), non-linear partial least square regression, neural networks, and genetic algorithms. Partial least squares regression is a chemometric method similar to principal component analysis. PLSR can be used to analyze spectra measurements from hundreds of wavelengths and to reduce them to a smaller number of components. The number of components necessary is selected by minimizing the root mean square error of prediction (RMSEP). \citet{Etheridge_2014} used PLSR to determine nutrient, dissolved organic carbon, and total suspended solids concentrations in brackish water.

Data Management Plan

All data created in this project will be stored in a data base. After the project is completed, all data, meta-data and analysis performed on the data will be stored in an R-package. The R- package will be publicly available for other researchers. 

Potential Pitfalls

Potential pitfalls include drought conditions that limit the opportunity to collect, treat, and analyze stormwater. If drought conditions persist through 2017-2018, synthetic stormwater will be used in the experiment. Synthetic stormwater is an accepted substitute for real stormwater that can be logistically difficult to collect and store \citep{henderson_removal_2007,hong_sustainable_2006}. Vandalism of the experimental unit is another pitfall. We plan to fence in the area to keep unauthorized persons for accessing. Finally, the proposed water quality sensor could fail to pick up contaminant pulses that the mussels react on. In that case we would still have valuable data from the mussel's gaping behavior, the lethal und sublethal effects of stormwater on mussels, as well as the treatment effect of bioretention media on these effects.

Intelectual Merit

Results from this project will improve our understanding of stormwater impacts on mussel species native to the Southeastern US. Mussel gaping data from the smart sensing stations will improve our understanding of stormwater pollutant transport in surface waters and effects on aquatic organisms. Based on this data it will be possible to prioritize stormwater mitigation efforts in highly urbanized watersheds. Finally, the water quality sensor could be implemented as a stand alone instrument - without the mussel gaping part - to monitor stormwater toxicity on a large spatial scale.
