Introduction

Nonpoint source pollution originating from stormwater is the main cause of surface water degradation in Alabama and the USA \citep{us-epa_assessed_2017}. Stormwater contaminants vary depending on the land use, i.e. urban, residential, industrial, agricultural, or forested areas. Urban land use increases impervious surfaces, such as roads, parking lots, and rooftops. These surfaces accumulate contaminants such as nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus), suspended solids, heavy metals (copper, zinc, lead, chromium, cadmium), and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (oils, gasoline).
Impervious surfaces do not infiltrate stormwater and conventional stormwater management directs contaminant-laden effluent to a storm sewer system that discharges directly to a stream, river, or lake. Alabama’s streams and rivers host the greatest biodiversity of freshwater mussels (~182 species) and crayfish (~83 species) in the world. Today, more than half of these species are threatened and declining. Degradation of water quality and habitat by urban use poses a major threat to remaining freshwater biodiversity – particularly in Alabama, but in other states as well.

Stormwater Treatment with Bioretention Media

Low impact development (LID), or green stormwater infrastructure, involves innovative stormwater management that treats the first, most polluted, flush of stormwater before it reaches a stream, river, or lake. Bioretention cells, a LID stormwater control measure, have been implemented in a small number of Alabama communities. Bioretention cells are constructed depressions in the landscape that capture and store stormwater for less than 48 hours (Figure 1). The components include an underlying specialized soil media that is specific to individual regions or states. In Alabama, the recommended soil media is 85-88% sand, 8-12 % fines, and 3-5% organic matter \citep{dylewski_lid_2014} . Recommended bioretention media in other states ranges in sand content from 30-90% with silts, clays, organic matter, and compost being the other constituents.
Figure 1: Cross section of typical bioretention cell (Alabama LID Handbook)
Bioretention cells treat stormwater by settling, filtration, and internal processes and research has shown wide ranging, but generally large (greater than 50%), reductions in concentrations of contaminants after treatment. Several authors report phosphorus removal, i.e. 60-80% \citep{davis_laboratory_2001}, > 65% \cite{davis_water_2003}, 67-83% \citep{chapman_performance_2010}, and 31% \citep{hunt_w._f._pollutant_2008} . Phosphorus leaching (especially soluble phosphorus) appeared to originate from fill media and surface mulch sources, especially with high organic or compost constituents, or from other decomposing organic sources such as grass clippings remaining in the facility \citep{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009,passeport_elodie_field_2009,hatt_hydrologic_2009}. Nitrogen reduction by bioretention cells ranged from 1 -82% for nitrate-nitrogen \cite{hunt_w._f._pollutant_2008,chapman_performance_2010} , 2-82% for ammonia \citep{davis_laboratory_2001,dietz_field_2005} , and 26-80% for total Kjeldahl nitrogen \cite{dietz_field_2005,davis_water_2006,davis_laboratory_2001} .
Total suspended solids (TSS) reduction rates of 99, 98, 96, 92, and 93% were reported by \citet{debusk_k._m._storm-water_2011}, \citet{glass_evaluation_2005}, \citet{roseen_storm_2006}, \citet{g._m._rusciano_bioretention_2007}, and \citet{chapman_performance_2010}, respectively. Reduction in TSS appears to be one of the most consistently improved water quality parameters in LID bioretention treatment \citep{brown_robert_a._impacts_2011}. \citet{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009} found heavy metals, both dissolved and particulate-bound, were efficiently removed in bioretention facilities, and that most of the removal occurs in the upper layers of the media \cite{li_heavy_2008}.
\citet{diblasi_removal_2009} noted that polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons were rapidly adsorbed and transported only a few centimeters beneath the surface in a bioretention facility, and did not pose a hazardous waste threat to either solid waste disposal of surface sediments or to groundwater; use of special sorbents is unnecessary for PAHs in bioretention facilities. Oil and grease (including motor oils) are a substantial contaminant in road surface storm runoff. Like metals, oil and grease is removed through bioretention facilities \cite{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009}. \citet{chapman_performance_2010} found consistently low minimum outflow concentrations of motor oil after treatment through their project facility.
Although research has been conducted to evaluate nutrient removal of bioretention cells in Alabama \cite{christian_effect_2012,mark_dougherty_evaluation_2007}, there is no information on contaminants such as metals and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. More research on bioretention cells in Alabama soils, climate, and stormwater is needed to evaluate the pollutant removal effectiveness of the recommended soil media.

Stormwater Effects on Aquatic Organisms

Stormwater typically contains a wide array of contaminants such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), metals, and pesticides. Runoff transports contaminants to receiving waters, many of which are toxic to fish \citep{skinner_developmental_1999,kayhanian_toxicity_2008,corsi_fresh_2010}, invertebrates \citep{hall_toxicity_1988,marsalek_exploratory_1999,kayhanian_toxicity_2008,corsi_fresh_2010}, and aquatic plants \cite{kayhanian_toxicity_2008}. In the western U.S., research on coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch), daphnia (Ceriodaphnia dubia), mayfly (Baetis spp.), and zebra fish (Danio rerio) that were exposed to untreated stormwater revealed devastating lethal and sublethal effects on these organisms \citep{mcintyre_zebrafish_2014,mcintyre_soil_2015,spromberg_coho_2016}. Untreated runoff caused heart failure, delayed hatching, reduced growth, pericardial edema, microphthalmia (small eyes), and reduced swim bladder inflation in zebra fish \citep{mcintyre_zebrafish_2014}. Untreated stormwater runoff was generally lethal to salmon and invertebrates and sublethal reproductive toxicity was detected in daphnia \citep{mcintyre_soil_2015}. Untreated stormwater runoff collected during nine storms was universally lethal to adult coho salmon \citep{spromberg_coho_2016} .
In contrast, lethal and sublethal effects decreased or were un-detectible after the stormwater was treated by flushing through a bioretention cell: Using a bioretention soil medium that was composed of 60% sand, 15% compost, 15% shredded bark, and 10% drinking water treatment residuals by volume, the bioretention treatment dramatically improved stormwater quality and reversed nearly all forms of developmental toxicity \citep{mcintyre_zebrafish_2014}. Similarly, bioretention soil media reduced or eliminate toxicity to juvenile Coho salmon as well as their macroinvertebrate prey, including cultured daphnia and wild-collected mayfly \citep{mcintyre_soil_2015}. Stormwater flushed through a soil medium containing 60% sand and 40% compost was not toxic to Coho salmon and mortality was prevented by the stormwater treatment. However, we have not found research that focuses on the Alabama bioretention soil mix (85% sand, 10% fines, 5% organics) and aquatic organisms that are native to Alabama and the Southeastern U.S.

Biological Early Warning Systems

Biological early warning systems provide behavioral and physiological response alerts when contaminants pulse down waterways in concentrations that are likely to have negative effects on aquatic communities \citep{bae2014biological}. Behavioral responses of bivalves can serve as a valuable early warning system. Mussels must keep their shells open to obtain food and oxygen from the surrounding waters. However, they respond to toxicants and close their shells. Electromagnetic sensors attached to each valve are used to monitor gaping and closing behavior and set off alarms when behavior indicates the presence of stressful toxicants in the water \citep{manley_behavioural_1979,kramer_valve_1989,gnyubkin_early_2009}. Systems such as the Mosselmonitor (www.mosselmonitor.nl; \citealt{kramer_valve_1989} ) and the Dreissena Monitor (Envicontrol Köln Germany; \citealt{borcherding_dreissena-monitor_1994}) have been used to monitor fresh and saltwater environments in Europe.
Effects of toxicants and other stressors on organisms can be divided into two categories: lethal and sublethal. Lethal effects result in death after a certain period of exposure to a specific concentration. However, a given stressor may have a detrimental effect without causing mortality. This is termed a sublethal effect. Sublethal effects are wide ranging and include reduced growth and reproduction, behavioral anomalies, and altered metabolic rates. Respiration rates of mussels and crayfish can be altered by metals and nitrogenous wastes \citep{meade_toxicity_1995,rowe_metabolic_2001,wang_metal_2005}. This in turn can strongly alter energetic budgets of organisms \citep{mueller_regulation_2011}, and lead to strong effects on scope for growth . Jim Stoeckel is currently conducting research to assess how thermal and chemical stressors affect metabolic patterns of mussels. In the proposed study, we will compare effects of treated and untreated stormwater on mussels and crayfish using a combination of lethal effect assays focusing on mortality, and sublethal assays focusing on respiration rates as a sensitive indicator of sublethal metabolic stress that can lead to changes in an individual’s scope for growth (Figure 2). We have found no research on the response of Alabama native mussels to treated or untreated stormwater.

Printed Circuit Board Environmental Sensors 

Commercial printed circuit board (PCB) technology is being used to realize low- cost environmental sensors. The non-conductive FR-4 substrate forms a rigid backbone for the sensing element. The patterned Cu cladding is used for sensing electrodes and signal traces. The polymeric solder mask serves as a thin insulating and moisture barrier layer. Four electrodes covered with solder mask are on the top side, where the solder mask electrically insulates them from the sensing environment. These electrodes can be used to measure the electrical permittivity or the electrical permeability of the surrounding media. Two exposed electrodes are shown on the bottom side which can be used to measure the resistance and therefore the electrical conductivity of the surrounding media. Through measuring these electrical properties of the surrounding environment, useful information about the environment can be ascertained, such as salinity \citep{Dean_2016}, soil moisture content \citep{Dean_2010}, nitrate concentration \citep{Yunus_2011}, pH \citep{Zoolfakar_2010}, water supply contamination \citep{Amri_Md_Yunus_2015},and the mass of a drop of water \citep{Meng_2016}. 

Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy 

Although there are many applications for environmental sensors that measure the impedance of a sample at a single frequency, additional information can sometimes be obtained by measuring the impedance at multiple frequencies or even over a wide range of frequencies. In environmental sensing applications, the sample under test is often an aqueous solution containing unknown contaminants, where it would be useful to determine what the contaminants are. Some chemicals that dissolve in or mix with water not only change the electrical properties of the water, but also change the electrical properties as a function of frequency, thereby creating an impedance versus frequency signature that can sometimes be used to identify the chemicals present in the aqueous solution. This type of analysis is called electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS).
Water is one of the most studied compounds. It is a polar molecule with an electrical resistivity of approximately 18.18 MΩ-cm at 25C \citep{Light_2005}, and a relative permittivity of approximately 78.3 at 25oC \citep{Schwan_1976}. The electrical properties of water are temperature dependent \citep{Malmberg_1956}. When an ionic compound dissolves in water, the ions become mobile charge carriers, which results in a decrease in the low frequency electrical resistivity of the aqueous solution, since ions move much slower in a solution than electrons move in a solid conductor. Additionally, dissolved ions, such as the nitrate ion, form physically large and concentration dependent hydration shells with water molecules. Due to their large size, these hydration shells possess a relatively low mechanical resonant frequency, which can be excited into vibration by applying RF energy at relatively low frequencies, on the order of tens of MHz \citep{Chighladze_2010}. This electromechanical phenomenon, known as relaxation, results in a frequency dependent complex permittivity. Other compounds, with much lower dielectric constants than water, can mix with water to alter the dielectric constant of the resulting aqueous solution. These effects cause the sensor’s impedance to be a function of both the chemistry of the solution and the excitation frequency at which the sensor’s impedance is measured.

Measuring Impedance as a Function of Frequency

A vector network analyzer can be used to measure impedance as a function of frequency. This instrument will excite the unknown complex impedance \(Z_L\), with an RF sinusoidal signal at a set amplitude and frequency through a known output impedance \(Z_0\). A real impedance of 50Ω is a typical value for \(Z_0\). If \(Z_L\) differs from \(Z_0\), part of the transmitted signal will be reflected back to the transmitting instrument, with its amplitude and phase dependent on the relationship between \(Z_L\) and \(Z_0\) according to:        
\(\bar{\rho_V}=\frac{\bar{V_r}}{\bar{V_i}}=\frac{\bar{Z_L}-Z_0}{\bar{Z_L}+Z_0}\)
