Stormwater runoff transports contaminants to receiving waters, many of which are toxic to fish \citep{skinner_developmental_1999,kayhanian_toxicity_2008,corsi_fresh_2010}, invertebrates \citep{hall_toxicity_1988,marsalek_exploratory_1999,kayhanian_toxicity_2008,corsi_fresh_2010}, and aquatic plants \cite{kayhanian_toxicity_2008}. In the western U.S., research on coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch), daphnia (Ceriodaphnia dubia), mayfly (Baetis spp.), and zebra fish (Danio rerio), that were exposed to untreated stormwater, revealed devastating lethal and sublethal effects on these organisms \citep{mcintyre_zebrafish_2014,mcintyre_soil_2015,spromberg_coho_2016}. Untreated runoff caused heart failure, delayed hatching, reduced growth, pericardial edema, microphthalmia (small eyes), and reduced swim bladder inflation in zebra fish \citep{mcintyre_zebrafish_2014}. Untreated stormwater runoff was generally lethal to salmon and invertebrates and sublethal reproductive toxicity was detected in daphnia \citep{mcintyre_soil_2015}. Untreated stormwater runoff collected during nine storms was universally lethal to adult coho salmon \citep{spromberg_coho_2016}.
