Stormwater runoff transports contaminants to receiving waters, many of which are toxic to fish (Skinner et al., 1999; Kayhanian et al., 2008; Corsi et al., 2010), invertebrates (Hall et al., 1988; Marsalek et al., 1999; Kayhanian et al., 2008; Corsi et al., 2010), and aquatic plants (Kayhanian 2008). In the western U.S., research on coho salmon (Oncorhynchus kisutch), daphnia (Ceriodaphnia dubia), mayfly (Baetis spp.), and zebra fish (Danio rerio), that were exposed to untreated stormwater, revealed devastating lethal and sublethal effects on these organisms (McIntyre et al., 2014; McIntyre et al., 2015; Spromberg et al., 2016). Untreated runoff caused heart failure, delayed hatching, reduced growth, pericardial edema, microphthalmia (small eyes), and reduced swim bladder inflation in zebra fish (McIntyre et al., 2014). Untreated stormwater runoff was generally lethal to salmon and invertebrates and sublethal reproductive toxicity was detected in daphnia (McIntyre et al., 2015). Untreated stormwater runoff collected during nine storms was universally lethal to adult coho salmon (Spromberg et al., 2016).
