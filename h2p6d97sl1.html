Bioretention cells treat stormwater and research has shown wide ranging, but generally large (greater than 50%), reductions in concentrations of contaminants after treatment: Several authors report phosphorus removal, i.e. 60-80% \citep{davis_laboratory_2001}, > 65% \cite{davis_water_2003}, 67-83% \citep{chapman_performance_2010}, and 31% \citep{hunt_w._f._pollutant_2008}. Phosphorus leaching (especially soluble phosphorus) appeared to originate from fill media and surface mulch sources, especially with high organic or compost constituents, or from other decomposing organic sources such as grass clippings remaining in the facility \citep{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009,passeport_elodie_field_2009,hatt_hydrologic_2009}. Nitrogen reduction by bioretention cells ranged from 1-82% for nitrate-nitrogen \cite{hunt_w._f._pollutant_2008,chapman_performance_2010} , 2-82% for ammonia \citep{davis_laboratory_2001,dietz_field_2005} , and 26-80% for total Kjeldahl nitrogen \cite{dietz_field_2005,davis_water_2006,davis_laboratory_2001}.
Total suspended solids (TSS) reduction rates of 99, 98, 96, 92, and 93% were reported by \citet{debusk_k._m._storm-water_2011}, \citet{glass_evaluation_2005}, \citet{roseen_storm_2006}, \citet{g._m._rusciano_bioretention_2007}, and \citet{chapman_performance_2010}, respectively. Reduction in TSS appears to be one of the most consistently improved water quality parameters in LID bioretention treatment \citep{brown_robert_a._impacts_2011}. \citet{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009} found heavy metals, both dissolved and particulate-bound, were efficiently removed in bioretention facilities, and that most of the removal occurs in the upper layers of the media \cite{li_heavy_2008}.
\citet{diblasi_removal_2009} noted that polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons were rapidly adsorbed and transported only a few centimeters beneath the surface in a bioretention facility, and did not pose a hazardous waste threat to either solid waste disposal of surface sediments or to groundwater; use of special sorbents is unnecessary for PAHs in bioretention facilities. Oil and grease (including motor oils) are a substantial contaminant in road surface storm runoff. Like metals, oil and grease is removed through bioretention facilities \cite{davis_allen_p._bioretention_2009}. \citet{chapman_performance_2010} found consistently low minimum outflow concentrations of motor oil after treatment through their project facility.
Although research has been conducted to evaluate nutrient removal of bioretention cells in Alabama \citep{christian_effect_2012,mark_dougherty_evaluation_2007}, there is no information on contaminants such as metals and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. More research on bioretention cells in Alabama soils, climate, and stormwater is needed to evaluate the pollutant removal effectiveness of the recommended soil media.
